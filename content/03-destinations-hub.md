# **Tiger Reserves in India**

*Eleven parks. Six regions. One job: pick the right one for you.*

India officially gazettes more than fifty **tiger reserves**, holding roughly 3,000 wild tigers (more than half the world's total). We work in eleven of them. Not because the others are bad, but because these are the ones we know properly, the ones with the lodges we trust, and the ones where the guides we hire have walked the same trails for fifteen years.

This is the pillar page for those eleven. If you are wondering which is the **best tiger reserve in India** has, the honest answer is that there is no single best. There is the best one for what you actually want, and the rest of this page will help you figure out which one that is.

## How to Choose

Most guests pick a park before they understand what they are choosing between. That is fine. But here is the short version, in case it helps.

- **Best odds of seeing a tiger:** Bandhavgarh, then Tadoba.
- **Closest to Delhi:** Ranthambore (5 hours by road) and Corbett (6 hours).
- **Closest to Mumbai or Pune:** Tadoba (fly to Nagpur, drive 3 hours).
- **Best for first-timers:** Kanha. The classic Central Indian experience and the safest bet for your first trip.
- **Best for families with young kids:** Pench. Beautiful, relaxed, and the lodges are good with children.
- **Best for photographers:** Bandhavgarh for the density, Ranthambore for the fort backdrops, Kanha for the meadow light.
- **Best for the trip nobody else has done:** Satpura. Walking safaris and boat safaris and almost no jeeps.
- **Lions, not tigers:** Gir. The only place on Earth with wild Asiatic lions.
- **Rhinos and elephants:** Kaziranga.
- **The truly remote one:** Manas, on the Bhutan border. Birding paradise.
- **The conservation comeback story:** Panna, where the tigers were locally extinct fifteen years ago and are now thriving again.

If your priority is sighting probability, you want a Central Indian park. If your priority is logistics from a particular city, the answer depends on the city. If your priority is doing something nobody at the dinner party has done, go to Satpura or Manas. Tell us which of these matters to you and we will pick for you.

## Central India

The tiger heartland. Six reserves, the highest tiger density in the country, and the lodges that the rest of India tends to copy. If you are doing one trip and you want it to count, this is the region you want. Most of the **best tiger safari in India** itineraries we run come from these six parks, and most of our repeat guests started their JJ journey here.

### **[Kanha National Park](/destination/kanha/)**

**Tagline:** The Land of Mowgli
**Tiger sighting:** 5/5
**Best for:** First-timers, meadow light, the classic experience
**Nearest airport:** Jabalpur (160 km, 4 hours)
**Best season:** October to June

Kanha is what Mowgli's forest looked like before the marketing department got involved. Sal trees in straight ranks, meadows wide enough that you forget what you came here for, and a tiger population that has been steady for two decades. Kipling never set foot here, but he read three books written by men who had, and the topography lined up. Bears are still here. Mowgli is, regrettably, fictional.

### **[Tadoba Andhari Tiger Reserve](/destination/tadoba/)**

**Tagline:** Maharashtra's Tiger Paradise
**Tiger sighting:** 5/5
**Best for:** Weekend escapes from Mumbai and Pune, bold close encounters
**Nearest airport:** Nagpur (150 km, 3 hours)
**Best season:** October to June

Tadoba is the closest tiger reserve to Mumbai and Pune, and it is the best **safari near mumbai** by a long way. Open terrain, bold tigers that walk past jeeps without breaking stride, and sighting rates that quietly embarrass the more famous parks. Three hours from Nagpur airport, two and a half nights from Friday evening to Monday morning, and the kind of weekend that has you booking the next one before you have unpacked.

### **[Bandhavgarh National Park](/destination/bandhavgarh/)**

**Tagline:** Highest Tiger Density in India
**Tiger sighting:** 5/5
**Best for:** Best sighting odds, photography
**Nearest airport:** Jabalpur (200 km, 4 hours)
**Best season:** October to June

Bandhavgarh has the highest tiger density in India, which is a polite way of saying you will probably see one before lunch on day one. Most of our guests do. The park sits on a stretch of sal forest, grassland, and an old hill fort that the locals will tell you is two thousand years old (it is closer to a thousand, but either way, old enough). The tigers here are the most photographed tigers on Earth.

### **[Pench National Park](/destination/pench/)**

**Tagline:** The Original Jungle Book
**Tiger sighting:** 4/5
**Best for:** Families, leopards, wild dogs
**Nearest airport:** Nagpur (100 km, 2 hours)
**Best season:** October to June

Pench is the actual Jungle Book setting, although Kipling never set foot in it and is therefore not strictly qualified to comment. Two hours from Nagpur, relaxed and beautiful, and the only park where you have a serious chance at seeing wild dogs hunting. Good with children, which is not something you can say about every reserve. Tigers, leopards, and the kind of Central Indian forest that does not bother showing off.

### **[Satpura National Park](/destination/satpura/)**

**Tagline:** Walking and Boat Safaris
**Tiger sighting:** 3/5
**Best for:** People who have done the famous parks already
**Nearest airport:** Bhopal (150 km, 4 hours)
**Best season:** October to April

Satpura is the **wildlife sanctuary in India** trip for guests who want something quieter. Walking safaris and boat safaris and almost no jeeps. The sighting rate is lower than the famous parks, which is the entire point: Satpura is for people who have done Kanha and Bandhavgarh and want to walk through a forest on foot for once. Sloth bears, gaur, leopards, and the occasional tiger when she feels like it.

### **[Panna National Park](/destination/panna/)**

**Tagline:** The Tiger Comeback Story
**Tiger sighting:** 4/5
**Best for:** Conservation interest, cultural pairings
**Nearest airport:** Khajuraho (25 km, 1 hour)
**Best season:** October to June

Panna lost every tiger it had in 2009. The reintroduction programme that followed is one of the few unambiguously good news stories in Indian wildlife conservation, and the park is now thriving again. It is also twenty-five kilometres from the Khajuraho temples, which makes it the easiest park to pair with serious cultural sightseeing. Vultures, gorges, and the Ken River.

## North India

### **[Ranthambore National Park](/destination/ranthambore/)**

**Tagline:** Tigers Among Ancient Ruins
**Tiger sighting:** 5/5
**Best for:** Iconic experience, photography, accessibility from Delhi
**Nearest airport:** Jaipur (180 km, 3.5 hours)
**Best season:** October to June

The most photographed tigers on Earth live in Ranthambore, and Ranthambore is the easiest **safari near delhi** by a long way. Tigers walking through the ruins of a tenth-century fort, lit by the kind of golden hour photographers travel years to find. Five hours from Delhi by road, or a 1-hour flight to Jaipur followed by a 3.5-hour drive. The park is iconic for a reason, and the reason is genuinely good.

### **[Jim Corbett National Park](/destination/corbett/)**

**Tagline:** India's First National Park
**Tiger sighting:** 4/5
**Best for:** Wild elephants, history, the second Delhi-adjacent option
**Nearest airport:** Pantnagar (80 km, 2 hours)
**Best season:** November to June

India's first national park, established in 1936. The reserve where the term "Project Tiger" was effectively invented. Sees fewer guaranteed sightings than the Central Indian parks but compensates with wild elephants, river landscapes, and the kind of historical weight no other reserve in India has. Six hours from Delhi by road, which makes Corbett the second viable option for guests who have already done Ranthambore.

## Northeast India

### **[Kaziranga National Park](/destination/kaziranga/)**

**Tagline:** Kingdom of the One-Horned Rhino
**Tiger sighting:** 2/5 (tigers are present but rarely seen)
**Best for:** Rhinos, elephants, birding
**Nearest airport:** Jorhat (100 km, 2.5 hours)
**Best season:** November to April

Kaziranga holds two-thirds of the world's wild one-horned rhinos. Roughly 2,400 of them. Wild elephants in herds of fifty. Water buffalo. Birds in numbers that will make a casual birder reconsider their hobby. The tigers are there, technically, but the grass is too tall to see them. You come to Kaziranga for everything else, and the everything else is staggering.

### **[Manas National Park](/destination/manas/)**

**Tagline:** UNESCO World Heritage
**Tiger sighting:** 2/5
**Best for:** Birding, the truly remote experience
**Nearest airport:** Guwahati (140 km, 4 hours)
**Best season:** November to April

The remote one. Manas sits on the Bhutan border, and it is a UNESCO World Heritage site for good reason. Rare golden langurs. The pygmy hog, which exists only here. Birds nobody has seen anywhere else. The park is for guests who want their **jungle safari in India** experience to feel genuinely off the map. It does, and it is.

## West India

### **[Gir National Park](/destination/gir/)**

**Tagline:** Home of the Asiatic Lion
**Tiger sighting:** N/A (lions, not tigers)
**Best for:** The only Asiatic lions on Earth
**Nearest airport:** Rajkot (160 km, 4 hours)
**Best season:** December to June

Gir is the only place on the planet where you can see wild Asiatic lions. Roughly 600 of them, all in this one forest. They were down to twenty individuals a century ago. The conservation story is one of the great recoveries, and the lions themselves are notably calm around vehicles in a way that makes a Gir safari feel completely different from the African version.
